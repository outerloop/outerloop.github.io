Startup Weekend Toronto EDU: Library Edition 2014

Apr 2nd, 2014

Thanks to everyone who completed the library survey. If you’re curious how your feedback was used, last weekend I participated in Startup Weekend Toronto EDU: Library Edition. Over the weekend, our impromptu team took an elevator pitch (SignWave) through business model development, market validation, prototype design and creation, and a final pitch to a panel of industry judges.

SignWave is an interactive digital sign for libraries. We identified two problems — students who have difficulty navigating academic libraries and librarians who are constantly answering directional queries. Our solution is to improve the current static signage by allowing library patrons to interact with large format signs via gestures to get directions and/or information, liberating librarians from answering repetitive queries.

It’s a crowded market for digital signs so we cater to the limited budgets of libraries by offering a free/low-cost solution using off-the-shelf parts and our open source signage software. Our company generates revenue by offering “Signs-as-a-Service” (i.e. hosting and support) and professional services (e.g. consulting, training).

You can play with the demo and flip through our deck here: http://signwave.github.io

Benford’s Law in the Wild

Mar 20th, 2014

A colleague at work mentioned something that turned out to be really cool — Benford’s Law. Also known as the First-Digit Law, it refers to the frequency distribution of digits in many real-life sources of data.

According to ths law, this is what the distribution of first digits looks like:

There are some caveats about the data that this distribution can be applied to. Namely, the data must be distributed across multiple orders of magnitude. That is, if the numbers only fall between 100 and 999, the law will not apply.

I had to see whether this law held true with my own eyes. Fortunately, in my day job, I work with lots of real-life data that is applicable to Benford’s law — trade data from trading desks at a bank.

Yup. It works!

One application of this law is (simplistic) fraud detection. As Phil Galfond once said (I think… can’t find the source to confirm), humans are bad at being random. Ask someone to pick a series of random numbers and you typically end up with a uniform distribution because “being random” means “try not to repeat digits”.

This Is Not the Beginning of a Blog

Feb 23rd, 2014

I’ve been writing and compiling notes to myself for a long time hence this is not the beginning of a blog per se. As much as I enjoy jotting thoughts in my Moleskine/Evernote/Gmail, knowing that the audience will likely be only myself doesn’t always lend itself to clarity and structure in writing. This is one reason why I’ve decided to share my thoughts and ideas with the universe — writing with a potential audience greater than one reader will encourage logical and persuasive writing. The other reason is to be part of the discussions, whatever they may be.
